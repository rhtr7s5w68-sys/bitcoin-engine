▮ The cost of security

Why mining uses energy

Mining is not free, and that is the point. The real-world cost is part of how the network protects itself.

What the energy buys

  • Mining requires real-world electricity and specialized equipment.
  • That cost makes honest mining the profitable choice.
  • Rewriting history would mean out-computing the entire network at once — enormously expensive.
  • So the energy spent is what makes the ledger hard to attack.

An honest tradeoff

Bitcoin's energy use is real, and people genuinely debate whether it is worth it.

Some point to mining powered by surplus or renewable energy. Others worry about total consumption and emissions. Both concerns are part of the conversation.

The balanced view: the energy is not wasted noise or a flaw to hide — it is the security budget. Whether that tradeoff is worth it is a fair thing to debate.
▮ Fixed supply

Bitcoin has a hard limit

Unlike traditional money systems, Bitcoin has a supply schedule written into its rules. The maximum supply is 21 million.

▮ The halving

The reward keeps getting cut in half

About every four years, the new Bitcoin paid to miners is cut in half. This slowly reduces how much new Bitcoin is created — until new issuance approaches zero.
